Gastrointestinal Cancers Kill
~40 Australians Per Day

Gastrointestinal cancer refers to a family of cancers (conditions of abnormal cell growth) that affect the digestive system. Gastrointestinal cancer is one of the most common form of cancer and it impacts both men and women. Annually, approximately 30,000 Australians are diagnosed with some form of gastrointestinal cancer.

Specific types of gastrointestinal cancers include:



  • Stomach (gastric) cancer

  • Bowel (colorectal) cancer

  • Liver cancer

  • Duodenal cancer

  • Bile duct cancer

  • Biliary cancer

  • Gallbladder cancer

  • Oesophagul cancer

  • Pancreatic cancer

  • Small intestinal cancer

  • Rectal cancer

  • Anal cancer





Source: AGITG, 2021

Risk Factors and Prevention

Factors that may increase your risk of gastrointestinal cancer includes:



  • Smoking tobacco

  • Age over 60

  • Alcohol consumption

  • Overweight or obese

  • Lack of exercise

  • Family history of gastrointestinal cancer

  • Diet high in smoked, pickled and salted foods
    and low in fruit and vegetables

  • Infection with the bacteria Helicobacter pylori

  • Previous stomach surgery

  • Pernicious anaemia (low red blood cells)

  • Chronic gastritis (inflammation of the stomach)


Having one or more of these risk factors does not guarantee you will get gastrointestinal cancer. Some of these risk factors can be controlled and some can not. We strongly advise you to avoid these risk factors to help reduce your chances of contracting some form of gastrointestinal cancer.








Source: Bowel Cancer Australia, 2021

Signs and Symptoms

Early-stage gastrointestinal cancer rarely cause symptoms.
Cases may vary but when the cancer does cause symptoms,
they may include the following:



  • Loss of appetite

  • Sense of fullness after only small meals

  • Abnormal weight loss

  • Unexplained Fatigue

  • Abdominal pain or discomfort

  • Heartburn or indigestion

  • Nausea

  • Vomitting with blood

  • Swelling or fluid build-up in the abdomen

  • Yellowing of the skin and eyes
    (if cancer spreads to liver)


These are symptoms generally found in gastrointestinal cancer patients.
While the symptoms may be caused by other types of cancer or diseases,
we strongly advise you to see a doctor if you have these symptoms, especially if they get worse, in order to find out the cause.








Source: Bowel Cancer Australia, 2021



Early Diagnosis and Treatment

Modern medicine cannot effectively treat many cancers, including gastrointestinal cancer, especially at its later stages. Therefore, early detection allows for more treatment options and often leads to higher survival rates. Some early cases of cancers may show signs and symptoms. However, that is not always the case.

Screening is a proactive testing for a disease, such as cancer, for people without symptoms. Routine screening is a practice used in certain places where gastrointestinal cancer is common. Depending on the person's level of risk, regular screening may or may not be worthwhile.

What you can do is to educate yourself further about gastrointestinal cancer and keep a check on your body. Based on the risk factors, if you identify yourself with a higher-than-average risk level, or feel worried and want to gain further clarification, we strongly advise you to go to a doctor and get professional advice on your condition and future steps to take.








Source: Bowel Cancer Australia, 2021
